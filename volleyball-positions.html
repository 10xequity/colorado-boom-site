Positions, Rotations & Court Layout

The Six Positions

Each spot has a job. Here's what your athlete is actually doing out there.

The primary attacker and the player who takes the most swings, hitting from the front-left and rotating through all six spots — so they pass, dig, attack, and block. Because the set travels farther to the pin, outsides need timing and the ability to read the block. Coaches recruit outsides who can do everything, not just hit.

Plays opposite the setter in the rotation and attacks from the front-right. Often the team's biggest blocker because they line up across from the opponent's outside hitter, and usually the second-leading attacker, getting the ball when the pass is too tight for the outside.

The offense's quarterback — takes the second contact on almost every play and decides who attacks. Setters need fast feet, soft hands, and quick decisions, and they disguise where the ball is going. When in the front row they're also active blockers.

The team's primary blocker on every front-row rally and the only player who slides to either pin to help block. On offense they hit fast, low “quick” sets and run slides. Middles are typically the tallest players, since blocking rewards height more than any other position.

A back-row defensive specialist in a contrasting jersey who can replace any back-row player without using a substitution — an unlimited swap made on dead balls. The libero can't attack a ball entirely above net height, can't block, and serves in only one rotation under USAV rules. Why the different jersey? So officials and the scorer can instantly tell the libero apart, because their substitution rules are unique.

A back-row player who plays defense and serves but, unlike the libero, is a normal substitute in a regular jersey and counts against the 15-per-set limit. A DS often comes in for a weaker passer or to serve a key run.

There's no height requirement. Height helps most at middle blocker, but liberos, defensive specialists, and setters succeed on quickness, ball control, and decision-making — shorter athletes thrive in those roles. Colorado Boom places players by skill and position fit, not height alone.

Rotations & Service Order

Why players seem to swap spots the instant the ball is served.

The six court spots are numbered 1 (back right, the server), 2 (front right), 3 (front middle), 4 (front left), 5 (back left), and 6 (back middle). When a team wins back the serve, everyone rotates one position clockwise and the player arriving at position 1 serves — and keeps serving until the team loses a rally.

Only at the instant the server contacts the ball. After that, players release to where they specialize — outsides slide left, the setter releases to the right-front to set, the libero slides to the best passing lane.

The most common rotation fault: at the moment of serve, two adjacent players are in the wrong order relative to each other. It costs an automatic point, so coaches drill starting alignments hard.

Substitution Rules

How players move on and off the floor.

USAV indoor allows 15 team substitutions per set. Once a substitute enters for a starter, that pair is linked — the starter can only re-enter for that same substitute. This differs from the NCAA/high-school 12-sub system.

Libero replacements are unlimited and don't count toward the 15. The libero comes in for a back-row player on a dead ball and comes out when that player would rotate to the front row.

No. The libero plays back row only. When they'd rotate to the front, they must come off and be replaced by the player they subbed for (or another back-row player). So a libero is on the floor for three of the six rotations.

Specialty Skills

The contacts and serves that make up a rally.

SkillWhat it is
Pass / BumpFirst contact off a serve or attack, usually forearms to the setter.
SetSecond contact, overhead with the fingers, delivering the ball to a hitter.
Attack / SpikeThird contact, a downward swing aimed at the opponent's floor.
Float serveA no-spin serve that moves unpredictably in the air.
Jump serveA serve attacked like a spike after a high toss.
Stuff blockA block that sends the ball straight back down for a point.
