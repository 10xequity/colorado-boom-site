Volleyball Rules Explained

The rules, scoring, and most-called faults in club volleyball — written for parents new to the sport.

The Basics

Two teams of six, three contacts a side, get the ball to the floor.

Volleyball is fast, but the framework is simple: two teams of six players share a court split by a net. Each team gets up to three contacts to send the ball back over, and you score by landing it on the opponent's floor (or forcing them into an error). A match is a best-of-five; sets are played to 25 points, and every rally scores a point — there's no need to be serving to score.

If you grew up with the older “side-out” game where only the serving team could score, today's rally-scoring system moves much faster. A single set rarely takes longer than 20–25 minutes.

Scoring & Match Length

How points, sets, and matches actually work.

Every rally results in a point for whichever team wins it — you no longer have to be serving to score. Matches are best-of-five sets. Sets 1–4 are played to 25 points (win by two); a deciding fifth set is played to 15 (win by two). There's no point cap, so a tight set can run past 25 until a team leads by two.

A typical three-set match runs about 60–90 minutes; a full five-set match is closer to two hours. Younger age groups often play shorter sets to 21 in pool play. There is no game clock — pace is set by the rallies, so plan to be at a tournament venue for most of the day.

The term survives from the old scoring system. Today it just means the receiving team won the rally, earns the point, and rotates one position before serving.

A kill is an attack that scores a point directly — it lands on the opponent's floor, can't be returned, or forces a blocking error. Kills are the primary offensive stat coaches track.

Common Faults

The calls you'll hear most from the sideline, in plain language.

A player contacts the net during play — hair, jersey, or a fingertip can all be called. The exception: if the ball is driven into the net hard enough to push it into a player, that's not a fault.

The ball comes to rest momentarily in the hands instead of being cleanly contacted. Most common on overhead sets that are too soft or held too long. Club-level refs call this more generously than higher-level officials.

One player contacts the ball twice in a row on what should be a single touch — often a set that spins out unevenly. Note: a double contact on the team's first contact (off a serve or hard-driven attack) is legal.

The server steps on or over the end line before contacting the ball. The serve must be made from completely behind the line.

At the moment the ball is served, each player must be in the correct order relative to their neighbors. If they're not — or the wrong player serves — it's a point for the other team.

A player's foot fully crosses the midline under the net and interferes with the opponent. A foot on the line is fine; entirely past it on the floor is a fault.

A back-row player may not take off from in front of the 3-meter (10-foot) line and contact the ball above net height. They can still hit it — they just have to jump from behind the line or contact the ball below the top of the net.

Each team gets a maximum of three contacts to return the ball (a block doesn't count as one). A fourth contact is an automatic point for the other team.

Trying to read the referee's calls from the stands? USA Volleyball publishes the official signal guide: USAV Officials' Hand Signals (Indoor).

Beach vs. Indoor — What's Different?

Colorado players cross over every summer, so the contrasts come up a lot.

Beach is played two-against-two on sand with no substitutions and no specialized positions — both players do everything. The court is slightly smaller (8 m x 8 m per side), matches are best-of-three sets to 21 (15 in the third), and players switch sides during the game to even out sun and wind. Hand-setting and open-hand tips are judged more strictly than indoors.

Quick Glossary

The words you'll hear from the bench and the stands.

TermWhat it means
AceA serve that scores directly — untouched, or not returnable.
AssistA set that leads directly to a kill.
DigA defensive contact (usually a forearm pass) that keeps an attack off the floor.
Dink / TipA soft, controlled attack dropped just over the block.
Free ballAn easy, controlled ball sent over because the other team couldn't attack.
KillAn attack that scores a point directly.
PancakeA flat-hand floor save that keeps the ball alive.
Side-outWinning the rally while receiving serve; earns the point and the serve.
Tool / WipeHitting the ball off the blocker's hands and out of bounds on purpose.
